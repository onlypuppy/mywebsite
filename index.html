1) Biography

Eminem, also known as Marshall Mathers, was born Oct 17, 1972 in St. Joseph Missouri. In the period of that night to today, he’s become a distinguished rapper and music producer. Nowadays recognized as one of the best-selling rappers, and as one of the best selling musical artists in history. He grew up in a broken household, and his parents separated around when Eminem was a year and a half old. Eminem grew up often switching houses, moving between different states, and mainly lived with family members. He grew up in an environment surrounded by poverty, violence, neglect, and drug use. Growing up in that kind of environment he became a victim, succumbing to the use of drugs at a young age. Eminem's album “Relapse” was created to express the idea in his mind of his personal journey through addiction and desperation. Eminem eventually came back into the music scene with this album after after a 5 year hiatus which marked his comeback after fighting with a heavy drug addiction. The way he was brought up contributed to songs spanning over his whole career, consisting of him mentioning his upbringing and how he felt growing up in a situation where he was constantly around drug use, introduced to drugs, and constantly in a violent setting, mentioned in his song “My Mom”.

5) Analysis of Concept Album

Eminem released relapse in 2009, he was out of the main spotlight for almost 5 years. One of the biggest parts of Marshall’s downward spiral was contributed by his childhood best friend and D12 groupmate, DeShaun “Proof” Holton’s death. In April 2006, Proof was shot and killed during an altercation at a Detroit nightclub. Proof was a very important factor in Eminem’s life, he’d encouraged Eminem to begin rapping, snuck him into rap battles, and served as his best man at his wedding. Marshall made a statement saying Proof’s death pushed him over the edge into depressive episodes and escalated his drug use from a crutch to a life-threatening dependency.


Eminem’s drug habit began during the production of his movie “8 mile” when Marshall was working 16 hour days, and started taking Ambien to sleep. By the time he went on hiatus, he was heavily hooked and dependent upon drugs. He was taking large amounts of prescription pills, those pills primarily being Vicodin, Valium, and Ambien—sometimes consuming anywhere from 60 to 90 pills a day. In trying to hide the pain, he began eating fast food persistently, leading his weight to skyrocket to around 230 pounds. He became relatively unrecognizable, which in turn slightly worked in his favor as he wasn’t noticed by fans often. Later, in 2005 Eminem was on the European part of his Anger Management 3 Tour. He unexpectedly cancelled the remaining dates, stating "exhaustion and other medical issues.” He checked into a rehab facility shortly after, but yielded unsuccessful results. Musically, he was almost entirely dormant. He released the greatest hits album Curtain Call: The Hits (2005) and a compilation album, Eminem Presents: The Re-Up (2006), to spotlight artists on his Shady Records label. During the time this was happening, he was suffering from writer’s block. He later made a statement speaking about the guest verses he did during this era. As stated by Eminem, he wrote and recorded them while heavily intoxicated, in the same statement, he mentioned he was deeply embarrassed by his actions. This dark era of Eminem’s life is what forged the album Relapse. When Eminem eventually went back to the studio with Dr. Dre, he didn’t just write up a new album; he created a horrorcore concept album. In order to cope with his trauma and the fact he needed to re-learn how to rap sober, Eminem brought back his alter ego, Slim Shady, from the grave. He didn’t bring back the prankster Shady we remember from the early 2000’s; he brought back a deranged, drug-fueled serial killer. The album is a literal and psychological journey through addiction, loss of sanity, a near-death overdose, and the complicated first steps towards recovery.


His story begins with the skit “Dr. West.” Eminem is having a conversation with his rehab counselor, who, rather than helping him, acts as an enabler, whispering temptations and eventually triggering Eminem’s relapse. The doctor’s voice quickly starts distorting into a demonic pitch, Eminem panics, transitioning into the album's first full track, “3 a.m.” The track thrusts the listener into the mind of a newly re-awakened, deranged alter-ego who is coming out of a violent, drug induced blackout. The song perfectly captures the severe disorientation of addiction: “It's 3 a.m. in the morning / Put my key in the door and / Bodies layin' all over the floor and / I don't remember how they got there.” On the surface, it plays out like a scene from a slasher film, psychologically, it shines light on the petrifying reality of substance abuse. The bodies and blood are a metaphor for waking up from a binge and seeing the destruction he caused his life and family, without any memory of doing anything. Eminem uses this exaggerated persona to hide the very real, terrifying loss of control he was feeling in his personal life.


After developing his monstrous state, the album explains why he is the way he is. In the following songs, “My Mom” and “Insane,” Eminem uses pretty dark humor to pull apart, and dig deep into the way he was brought up. On “My Mom,” he puts the blame on his mother, Debbie, for his addiction. He mentions early on in the song, “My Mom” that “Valium was in everything, food that I ate, the water that I drank, fuckin' peas in my plate, she sprinkled just enough of it to season my steak.” This is followed by “Insane,” a grotesquely exaggerated, unnerving track about severe childhood trauma and sexual abuse. While heavily fictionalized for shock value, these tracks detail Eminem exploring his trauma and heavily unhealthy family dynamics which laid the foundation for his collapse. On the track “Hello,” he portrays a man waking up from a multi-year blackout, unaware of how far gone he is. He raps “Allow me to introduce myself, my name is Shady, it's so nice to meet you / It's been a long time, sorry I've been away so long, my name is Shady.” He treats his return as a bender that he just stumbled out of, rather than a triumphant moment of his story.


This ends up leading to a string of tracks where the horrorcore concept reaches its peak. In “Same Song & Dance” and “Stay Wide Awake,” Eminem utilizes the serial killer persona as a huge, elaborate metaphor for the destructive nature of addiction. In “Same Song & Dance,” he describes abducting popstars such as Lindsay Lohan and Britney Spears, creating a hypnotic, methodical rhythm that mirrors the habits of a junkie getting ready for their next fix. “Stay Wide Awake” takes the horror imagery further, but still serves a vital purpose: It is Eminem proving to himself—and the listener—that his brain still works. Through complex, multi-syllabic rhyme schemes, (“Soon as my flow starts, I compose art like the ghost of Mozart” ), he uses the violent and grotesque imagery to mask his insecurities about relearning how to rap sober. He hides his pain behind a fictional, untouchable, monster.


For a short moment, the album makes an attempt at the “glory days” of the early 2000’s. The tracks “Old Time’s Sake” and “Must Be The Ganja” act as the “high” of the album. Eminem and Dr. Dre rap about smoking weed and taking pills with a celebratory “feeling” to it. It seems like it should be the “ultimate party,” a justification of his substance abuse. Nevertheless, in the context of the album, and its narrative, it’s a fake sense of security in that it’s going to come to an inevitable crash. That crash arrives in a calamity of an impact on the skit “Mr. Mathers.” The horror-movie comes to a screeching halt as we hear audio of two paramedics finding that Eminem had overdosed, and wasn’t responding to vocal cues. This skit carries on into the song “Deja vu,” which is the turning point and the main emotional anchor of Relapse. The alter-egos, serial killers, and shock value are stripped away. For the first time on the record, Marshall Mathers steps up to the microphone, vulnerable. He breaks down his addiction into his lyrics, meticulously detailing his addiction, the shame of hiding it from his daughters, and the terrifying reality of his overdose. He admits to the physical toll it took: “Go in the room and shut the bedroom door for / And wake up in an ambulance like, ‘How did I get here?’” and speaks about the lengths he went to hide his habit: “Hide my Valium inside of my rectum.” “Deja Vu” shatters the illusion of the “fun,” crazy drug-user that Shady embodied on the earlier tracks, revealing a broken, scared man trapped in a cycle he couldn’t break.


After the events of “Deja Vu,” there comes the track “Beautiful.” It was the only song on the album written and partially recorded while Eminem was actually in the darkness of his hiatus and addiction. Whilst the rest of the album looks back at his drug use through a sober lense, “Beautiful” puts the listener smack dab in the middle of his severe depression. He speaks to the listener, rapping “I just can't be myself, I don't know why... In my shoes, just to see what it's like, to be me / I'll be you, let's trade shoes, just to see what it'd be like.” It’s a moment of raw humanity that grounds the entire concept album, showing that underneath the layers, the mask, beneath all of that, there’s a man drowning in grief and losing his own identity.


As the album concludes, Eminem finally takes his first steps outside his shadows. “Crack A Bottle” acts as his personal feeling of victory, sharing it with Dr. Dre and 50 Cent, celebrating his survival and return to rap. The finale, “Underground,” has a chaotic beat where Eminem locks his serial killer persona away, and reverts to his original rap roots. We don’t get offered a perfect, happy ending; instead, we see Eminem’s escape from his demons through rap.


In the end, Relapse isn’t just a collection of shock-rap songs, it's a beautiful, twisted coping mechanism. By stepping into the shoes of a literal monster, Eminem was able to explore the monster he had become during his addiction. Throughout the whole album, starting with “Dr. West” to “Underground,” Eminem takes the listener through his story, relapsing, and surviving to tell the tale.
